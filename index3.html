The time when Godel was wrong.

Using his method, universe of contructibility, in which one may omit all arbitrary mathematical objects in order to create a consistent mathematical model to answer certain questions of solvability, Godel demonstrated how the Continuum Hypothesis is orthogonal to his incompleteness theorems. However, Cohen later came about and used his same method to demonstrate that the Continuum hypothesis may also be wrong. This led to the conclusion that our current mathematical tools are not sufficient. Godel blamed his shortcomings on his illness later in 1972.

The time when Hilbert was wrong.

Hilbert asked whether the question of there being an infinite number of points on a line to be possible to prove. It is either possible that there infinite number of points on a line or lines are self-containing and do not have the same infinity as the real (whole) and rational numbers. The question may have been a bad one, as it had been shown to better ask whether there are an infinite number of subsets on a line, rather than points, which makes the Continuum Hypothesis potentially more tractable.

The time when Frege was wrong

Frege defined a set as the collection of elements for which the element cannot be a member of itself. Russell later observed that this would lead to a contradiction and formulated his vicious circularity principles. If a set of all barbers cut their client's hair, there must exist a barber for which has to cut his own hair. Therefore this would lead to the contradiction that there must both be elements in a set describing this totality and also not. The conflict arrives when we attempt to describe it, we refer to the elements and not the totality itself. Godel showed how this simply means this applies in the realm of constructible mathematics, and not mathematics as it exists in the ontological realm.
